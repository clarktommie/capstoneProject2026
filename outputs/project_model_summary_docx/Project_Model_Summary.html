Project Model Summary

Prepared from visible notebook outputs only. No new visuals were created.

SECTION 1 — Overview

This project looks at school shooting patterns across U.S. states over time and compares those patterns with state firearm policies. The notebooks focus on two practical questions: whether policies are linked to how often incidents happen, and whether they are linked to how harmful those incidents are.

The analysis uses state-by-year data, public school enrollment, and policy indicators from 1987 forward. Across the notebooks, the clearest pattern is a strong rise in recent years, while most policy effects remain small or unclear in the main models.

SECTION 2 — Models Used

Negative Binomial Model for Incident Count

What it was used for: Measuring how often school shooting incidents happen in each state and year.

What question it answers: Are policy changes linked to more or fewer incidents?

Target variable: Incident count.

Negative Binomial Model for Total Victims

What it was used for: Measuring the total amount of harm across each state and year.

What question it answers: Are policy changes linked to more or fewer total victims?

Target variable: Total victims.

Gamma Model for Victims per Incident

What it was used for: Measuring how severe incidents are once an incident has already happened.

What question it answers: Are policy changes linked to higher or lower harm per incident?

Target variable: Victims per incident.

Poisson Model with State-Clustered Errors

What it was used for: Checking whether the same broad pattern still appears under a stricter comparison.

What question it answers: Do the overall policy and time patterns still point in the same direction?

Target variable: Incident count.

SECTION 3 — Key Findings

  • The strongest result is a national rise in incident rates in the later years, especially from 2018 through 2024.
  • The same late-year rise also appears when the outcome is total victims, not just incident count.
  • In the main incident-count model, none of the policy measures show clear evidence of a strong effect on their own.
  • In the total-victims model, the policy results are again mostly unclear.
  • In the victims-per-incident model, the K-12 settings policy measure stands out clearly and is linked to higher harm per incident.
  • The background check policy measure comes close in some results, but the notebook output does not show enough evidence to treat it as a firm conclusion.
  • The notebooks themselves describe the national time pattern as the strongest empirical finding and describe short-run policy effects as weak.

SECTION 5 — Final Takeaways

  • The clearest story in the notebooks is a nationwide increase in school shooting risk in recent years.
  • The notebooks do not show strong, consistent evidence that most single policies changed incident counts on their own.
  • The same is true when the outcome is total victims: recent time trends are clearer than most policy effects.
  • One policy measure stands out in the incident-only severity model, where the K-12 settings policy measure is linked to higher harm per incident.
  • For decision-making, the most important message is that time trends dominate the results shown in these notebooks.
